April Mixtape 3
Snow Strippers

To cap off April, where better to conclude than with what I believe to be the definitive Snow Strippers release, April Mixtape 3. Yes, April Mixtape 2 is more widely critically acclaimed. Yes, Night Killas Vol.1 gets a higher average score. Keeping that all in mind - I still see April Mixtape 3 as the cornerstone. It expands upon the shorter, narrower Night Killas EP, combining the rich instrumentation of [Stab The Voice](https://www.mwaterman.dev/music/Snow%20Strippers%20-%20Stab%20The%20Voice.md) with the viral appeal of [So What If I'm A Freak](https://www.mwaterman.dev/music/Snow%20Strippers%20-%20So%20What%20If%20I'm%20A%20Freak.md) - resulting in their best mixtape to date. With both halves of the duo in their prime, two genre-transcending highlights, and viral success, April Mixtape 3 is the best that the Snow Strippers have to offer.

I see April Mixtape 3 as the best version of their sound - otherworldly and ethereal, while remaining catchy and engaging - speaking to both of their strengths. Tati's voice works well as main vocals, and even better as it accompanies the melody and matches the percussion. For example, on I'm On My Own !SM, the matching of her vocals to the kicks is pleasantly rhythmic: the short syllables of the title; the chopping of "co-cold out there" and "every pers-person" in the verses. Similarly, on Again !SM, I love how her strained delivery of "Wanting you again" matches the rhythm. Even the small details later in the track - where the echo of the end of "Quietly bleed out in my sleep" pairs with cymbal hits - demonstrate consistent refinement. Building on tracks like Night Killas' [Cautious](https://www.mwaterman.dev/music/Snow%20Strippers%20-%20Night%20Killas%20Vol.%201.md?deeplink=Cautious), the best effect is where her vocals transform from matching the percussion to being the percussion. With her voice layered and echoed, the sibilance becomes percussive - the hiss of an S, the kick of a K, echoing syllables that fill the space. For example, on Deceitful Eyes !SM, where there are no hi-hats - just the echoing of her voice filling the top end. This reinforces the darker vibe - instead of bright, cleanly placed hi-hats, the role is filled by a fuzzier, echoing, more intimate placement of her vocals. At times, this can go too far - like on Your Ridicule !SM - where the track becomes too fuzzy, too hard to resolve. This track is nearly excellent, with her glowing vocalizations swirling over compressed, digital bass hits - but it falls short during the chorus. The combination of the vocals and cymbals becomes too sibilant, losing replay value, and it leaves the bridge with question marks where lyrics ought to be. Accepting this as the lone weak point for her vocals - April Mixtape 3 is packed near-constantly with textured and expansive vocal performances that define their sound.

This production-vocal synergy comes from the other half of the duo, Graham Perez, and his signature production. They've been called the most exciting act in electroclash since Crystal Castles, and it's obvious why Crystal Castles is a touchpoint. Beyond the similarity as a duo of duos, April Mixtape 3 is a clear continuation of the dark, witch-house-y electroclash that made Crystal Castles a legendary pairing. For example, despite being the least popular track, Depriving You !SM would easily fit alongside [Kerosene](https://open.spotify.com/track/6E1YebXpPPtujMUljDNlOo?si=c2de530509dd4e46) or [Vietnam](https://open.spotify.com/track/1KuorL0TWWgu4GZqnyv29K?si=e8a0cda9bafc45c5) with the pitched-up echolalia. On Fake Smile !SM, we get echoing screams, swirling sirens, and that icy, stuttering melody evokes a similar, chilling reaction to [Transgender](https://open.spotify.com/track/26ZzSLUJMmYp5u1hDELEWH?si=14e0f0325c124480). Finally, the aforementioned Deceitful Eyes strikes me as similarly crystal-castles-esque - the composition of the pulling bass, bitpop synth and interweaving of many vocal layers would fit perfectly on (II). Consider [Suffocation](https://open.spotify.com/track/12oxsGACfwMozi4nK9noGQ?si=443403ff81654a10), perhaps my favorite of their work - with its shimmering instrumental, haunting disposition, gentle and expansive vocals from Alice Glass - traits that make it exceptional among their work are common points of praise for Snow Strippers. Across all these tracks - they often trade a truly otherworldly, cryptic, hollowing palette for more danceable and approachable results; trading higher peaks and higher amplitude for a more coherent experience. Considering how many excellent songs are here - I think it's a worthy trade.

That's not to say there aren't high peaks here - April Mixtape contains both of my favorite Snow Strippers tracks, which for me truly transcend the genre. They're not "good electroclash-adjacent nightwave/witch house" - they're just fantastic tracks, without caveats or qualifiers. Although Under Your Spell is the one with the viral success, for me, the Snow Strippers' two best songs are Don't You Feel !SM and Lacerate !SM. I love Don't You Feel, with its pulsing bass, blown-out "Don't you feel // I'm a star", over the beaming synth-vocalizations pair that serve as the melody. Here, the echoing vocals are exceptional - "All the arms are crossed // Falling down the line". Her vocals are quite beautiful here, which is a rare trait for a song in this genre. Of course, its DNA shows very clearly, with the same laughing sample and heavy percussion that's present across all their songs. It exemplifies almost everything there is to love about the Snow Strippers, and for that, I adore the track. Similarly, Lacerate nails everything about their digital sound that I love - a spacious soundstage, astringent riser, digital bass, and synthetic chimes. Atop this instrumental platform, her vocals glow, layered and echoing, with the air in her syllables becoming percussive. For me, the song really peaks on the bridge, with the impassioned delivery "Cause I hate // All of it // All of you" echoing, matching the beat and enveloping the listener. I find the track extremely engaging, with the haunting repetition of 'lacerate // really lacerate' and "Aren't you alone? Really, aren't you alone?" as a fitting companion for a late-night listen. I've become doubly attached to this song after hearing it live - the dynamics of the room-shaking bass; the impossible clarity of the instrumental, even through earplugs. I hope I never lose the memory of how it sounded live. 

The mixtape is not just my favorite project from them, I'd argue it's the most important for their cultural impact and legacy. I see it as how they really "broke containment" going from 50,000 monthly listeners to over five million. Though most songs have one or two million plays, there are two with an order of magnitude more, both of which contribute to their growth. The first is It's A Dream !SM, with Lil Uzi Vert. No - it's not my favorite track, but this collaboration and fan cross-pollination is obviously beneficial to Snow Strippers. To this day, you'll see it on their Instagram comments, Genius annotations, and hear it triumphantly at shows - "IT'S JUST A DREAM!". The second, more recently, and even larger impact is Under Your Spell !SM, which has absolutely exploded. Had you asked me before this review how popular it was, I would remember it at under 2 million listens. At the time of writing, it's over a quarter of a billion. Though TikTok looksmaxxing propaganda and Dexter edits aren't how I'd hope for their music to reach a wider audience, it again exposes new people to their work. Putting aside its current cultural context, the song is legitimately excellent. The repeated 'oh-oh // ah-ah' and "Foreboding scars you can see // They're permanent, I am not" absolutely embossed this song into my brain on first listen. The lyricism, although limited, is very compelling. The idea that the scars may be permanent, that you are not - that physical harm may outlast the self - is quite gripping. It continues - "I want an innocent life // the rest to die" - willing to kill the self, literally or figuratively, to escape being under someone's spell is really quite haunting. The combination of an indelibly catchy beat and dark lyricism makes it clear why this is their watershed hit. There are other popular songs, other viral moments - but the two obvious touchpoints both land on this album - further positioning it as the cornerstone release for the duo.

With April Mixtape 3 driving their growth, providing their most viral hit, and containing both Don't You Feel and Lacerate, it's obvious why it's my favorite Snow Strippers project. I've been interrogating their discography all month, and my appreciation for it has only grown. More than that, though, the virality and lasting impact of this mixtape will be felt beyond just Snow Strippers fans. Both halves are already bleeding into new releases: glowing vocals, echoed plosives, and chopped syllables; icy instrumentation, expansive soundstages, ethereal mixing. If Crystal Castles was the foundation for this release, then April Mixtape 3 is the foundation for another decade of electroclash.
