On last week's review of So What If I'm A Freak, I discussed one end of Snow Strippers' range, as a catchy, sleeper hit made up of fun samples. This song, Stab the Voice, is the peak of the other end. Where So What If I'm A Freak is a straightforward single with many millions of plays, Stab The Voice is from a little-known YEAR0001 compilation album. Instead of serving as a mass-appeal gateway, Stab The Voice is less accessible, more haunting, more limited in its appeal. Though it may not be everyone's favorite upon first listen, I find Stab The Voice to be just as engaging, and it represents a more elevated, innovative direction for their music. 

Stab the Voice is innovative because of how it builds on previous work in this space: Snow Strippers have often been compared to Crystal Castles, and the comparison is apt. It's important to consider the difference between aping and innovating, and in my opinion, they're very much the latter. I find the sound palette to be evocative of old Crystal Castles - the melodic synthesizers, obviously synthetic percussion, and airy risers that all would fit in on a Crystal Castles track. That alone is a mark of quality - to build upon the work of another important duo - but I think there's material innovation here. On this track, I particularly like the bifurcated melody - on the right, you get the glassy mallet tones, and on the left you get the buzzy, almost nasal synth. The contrast between the two sounds: the electronic feel of the electrified, square-wave; the crystalline, glowing tones of the pitched percussion - is fantastic. Although I doubt Graham recruited a real xylophone here, the chimes' more natural timbre complements the otherwise fully digital sound. 

This spatial placement amplifies more than the individual textures - it builds the soundstage around the listener. This song resonates because it feels spacious and gripping, which requires real precision in the composition. From the very first moments - with the distant scream panning from ear to ear, the instrumental fills the whole soundstage. As the melody is overtaken by her echoing vocals, and a swirling arpeggio - the track continues to expand. Finally, it drops back into the chorus - "I will stab the voice // I can't take the voice" - with her voice front and center. The use of air is consistently excellent - swelling risers, exhaling falls, her audible inhales, fuzzy synths - all feel expansive and hollow. As the track progresses, it envelops the listener, as more elements layer and blend to fully consume the soundstage. 

Along with the audible inhales and clean placement, her vocals are the central element that completes the arrangement. Throughout the verses, you can hear just a touch of auto-tuning and layering as she steps between frequencies. Though in other genres, this might be distasteful, here it sharpens the corners, and amplifies the digital feel of the entire track. Outside of the verses, her voice is chopped, echoed, and panned. These chops stop being vocal and start feeling percussive - the breath in the echoing final S-sound of 'voice', the plosive of the start of 'Cause'. These wisps of her voice bring everything together, simultaneously acting like high-hats and bleeding her voice into the instrumental. Although a tiny detail, the track would feel markedly incomplete without it, and it's come to be a staple of their sound in later work. 

Stab The Voice, with its rich instrumentation, suffocating arrangement, and refined vocals, has become one of my favorite tracks from Graham and Tati. More than that, though, it is an important foundation for Snow Strippers' body of work. This track builds on Crystal Castles' legacy of witch-housey electroclash, blending dark, ethereal textures into catchy tracks. Its echoing vocal snippets set the stage for Night Killaz Vol 1.'s "Cautious", or April Mixtape 3's "Don't You Feel". Though it might not have the sheer replay value of So What If I'm A Freak, nor the play count of Under Your Spell, it is among the best of what Snow Strippers have to offer. 

Track Ratings:
Stab The Voice - 100
